Online appendix

Frequently asked questions accompanying One Drug, Many Prices: A Proposal to Reform US Healthcare Pricing. Back to overview.

These are the questions most commonly raised about the three reforms. They are answered here rather than in the body of the note so that readers can scan and expand only what interests them.

Is the proposed trio of reforms novel?

No, each of the three reforms has been studied, advocated, or partially implemented. What is new is the claim that they belong together, and the specific design that makes them enforceable as a single system. Reform 1 without Reform 2 produces a powerful buyer facing private-pay loopholes that erode its gains, which is the current situation. Reform 2 without Reform 1 imposes a uniform rate with no one empowered to set it low. Either without Reform 3 remains exposed to the secret side-deals and misreporting that have defeated past price-control efforts. The contribution of this note is to integrate the three.

Reform 1 (consolidated negotiation). It is well understood that the federal government leaves bargaining power on the table by fragmenting its purchasing across Medicare, Medicaid, the VA, the DoD, the IHS, and other programs. The CBO reports that prices paid by federal programs for identical drugs differ by wide margins, with the VA paying roughly half of Medicare Part D for selected drugs (CBO 2021a; GAO 2021). Gusmano et al. (2020) show that peer nations achieve lower fee-for-service prices precisely by negotiating as unified blocs. Consolidated purchasing works at smaller scales inside the US federal system: the Federal Supply Schedule serves the DoD, IHS, Federal Bureau of Prisons, and Public Health Service off VA-negotiated contracts (CBO 2021a), and state-level pooled purchasing coalitions such as the Sovereign States Drug Consortium extract supplemental Medicaid rebates beyond federal levels. Under the IRA, Medicare's first exercise of negotiation, confined to ten drugs, produced an estimated 22% in net savings on those drugs, according to CMS (KFF 2024). Reform 1 scales the same mechanism from a dozen fragmented programs to one Bloc covering every federal healthcare purchase.

Reform 2 (uniform pricing as a condition of market access). The case for all-payer pricing has been argued in the US health-policy literature for decades. Reinhardt's (2011) sustained critique of multi-price systems showed that price discrimination across payers generates opacity, cost-shifting claims that do not hold up empirically, and persistent exploitation of the weakest payers. Subsequent work confirmed the empirics: White (2013) and Frakt (2011) find little evidence that lower Medicare payments push private payments up, undercutting the industry's standard defense of the multi-price system. Anderson and Herring (2015) set out the design case for a US all-payer rate-setting framework drawing on the German, Swiss, and Japanese examples, and on Maryland's all-payer hospital system, which has operated since 1977. Emanuel et al. (2012) advanced a version of the same proposal in a widely-cited New England Journal of Medicine piece. Cooper et al. (2019) and RAND (2024) document the magnitude of what all-payer pricing would undo, namely private-insurer prices that run at roughly 2.5 times Medicare rates, with the largest gaps in concentrated hospital markets. Conditioning federal market access on pricing behavior is also not new: the 340B Drug Pricing Program requires manufacturers that participate in Medicaid to sell outpatient drugs to safety-net providers at roughly 25 to 50% below list, and the Medicaid Best Price rule requires manufacturers to offer Medicaid the lowest price offered to any non-federal purchaser. Both show that conditional-access mechanisms are workable and durable. Reform 2 combines the all-payer tradition with the conditional-access enforcement tool.

Reform 3 (price transparency). Empirical evaluations of price-transparency interventions consistently find price reductions where transparency is actually achieved. The CalPERS reference-pricing program, which covers California's public employees, reduced prices by 17 to 26% and shifted patient volume toward lower-priced facilities (Robinson and Brown 2013; Robinson, Brown, and Whaley 2015). New Hampshire's HealthCost tool produced modest price reductions for shoppable services, particularly imaging (Whaley 2019). Singapore publishes hospital-bill benchmarks for common procedures by provider and has induced visible price competition. These precedents reach only subsets of the market. The US Hospital Price Transparency Rule (2021) and Transparency in Coverage Rule (2020) are national in scope but have largely failed to deliver usable transparency because they delegate posting to thousands of providers and insurers and impose symbolic penalties as low as $300 per day, a trivial sum for a system billing millions (45 CFR 180.90). Reform 3 departs from the delegated-posting model: because every price is set in a Bloc contract, the Bloc is the natural publisher of record.

What is incremental vs. novel. The consolidation of federal purchasing (Reform 1) is an incremental extension of existing mechanisms like the Federal Supply Schedule. The all-payer and conditional-access tools in Reform 2 draw on an established tradition and on existing federal programs. The transparency component (Reform 3) is incremental in substance but novel in design, since it removes the weakest link (provider-side posting) from existing transparency regimes. The novel contribution is the integrated system, in which each reform closes a loophole the others leave open.

What is the advantage of SES over performance-based or salary pay?

Bloc negotiators are paid as SES employees: a fixed salary, a performance bonus capped at 10% of base, and no other monetary upside of any kind. Bonuses are awarded on periodic peer-reviewed evaluations rather than per-contract outcomes.

Two failure modes must be guarded against. The first is laxness: salaried employees with no upside may have weak incentives to bargain hard. The second is gaming: employees paid against measurable outcomes will optimize for those outcomes at the expense of unmeasured ones. For complex public-sector roles like Bloc negotiation, where many dimensions of performance (supply security, manufacturer participation, clinical quality, equity) are not measurable, the economics literature identifies the second failure mode as the more dangerous, warning that high-powered incentives can backfire when key dimensions are unmeasurable and that muted, low-powered incentives are often optimal (Holmström and Milgrom 1991; Prendergast 1999; Frey and Jegen 2001; Bénabou and Tirole 2003, 2006; Finan, Olken, and Pande 2017). The 10% bonus cap reflects a deliberate choice in the safer direction; structural anti-corruption controls (no outside payments, no industry employment for twelve months after tenure, public reporting of contracts) carry the load that high-powered incentives cannot.

How are providers deterred from overcharging patients?

Fines must be large enough that a provider considering a deliberate overcharge, or negligent about avoiding one, expects to pay more than they would gain. The challenge is that few patients are likely to notice or file complaints over modest overcharges. If fines are commensurate with the occasional complaints, providers can overcharge routinely, pay the occasional fine when caught, and still profit. Meaningful deterrence therefore requires a multiplier.

The multiplier should be large. To fix ideas, a multiplier of 100 would turn a $50 overcharge into a $5,000 fine, salient to the provider yet still small enough that a genuine overcharge is worth a complaint. The figure of 100 is only illustrative of the order of magnitude required, not a specific recommendation.

Fines alone are not enough. Repeat offenders should lose their Bloc contract entirely, which for most providers means losing access to the majority of the market. Termination, not the fine, is the real deterrent against systematic abuse. The fine handles single-instance overcharges; contract termination handles patterns.

How would these reforms change the healthcare-provision landscape?

Providers and hospitals could no longer maintain wide price gaps across payers. An average private insurer in 2022 paid 2.5 times the Medicare rate for hospital services, with the largest gaps in concentrated markets where a single hospital chain dominates (RAND 2024; Cooper et al. 2019). Under the Bloc, that gap would collapse and price discrimination across payers would disappear. Hospitals in concentrated markets would face the largest reductions, because they currently extract the largest markups. Provider margins on routine services would compress, but volume would stabilize and billing-and-collection overhead, much of which exists to sustain price discrimination, would shrink.

How would these reforms change the insurance market?

The insurance market would survive but would compete on different ground. Today, insurers compete partly by negotiating secret prices with providers. They also use narrow networks to steer patient volume toward providers who agreed to lower rates. Under Bloc-wide pricing, every contracting provider charges the same price to every payer. Narrow networks lose their rationale. No insurer can win a discount by restricting who its members can see. Competition shifts to coverage design. What fraction of the uniform price does each plan cover? What services are included? What is the premium, and how generous is the cost-sharing? Insurers remain distinct entities competing for enrollees. But the secret-price arbitrage that drives much of today's market disappears.

How would these reforms change the pharmaceutical payment structure?

The rebate ecosystem would cease to exist, and the drug supply chain would undergo an overhaul that eliminates many intermediaries. There would be no rebates to capture, no spread pricing to exploit, and no opaque price differentials to arbitrage. Pharmacy benefit managers, whose business model depends on these arrangements, would lose their function. The clinical role that PBMs sometimes fill, maintaining formularies and handling claims processing, is absorbed into the Bloc's negotiation and price-lookup infrastructure. Manufacturers, wholesalers, and pharmacies would continue to operate, but the economic rents extracted by intermediaries sitting between them would be eliminated.

Why would Bloc negotiation produce lower net prices than PBMs already achieve through rebates?

PBMs are known to aggressively negotiate rebates that bring net prices below list prices. Replacing PBMs with Bloc-wide negotiation would be advantageous for four reasons.

First, PBMs retain a portion of the rebates they negotiate from manufacturers rather than passing them all through to plans. The retained portion aggregates to a meaningful share of total drug spending and is not disclosed (Sood et al. 2017). Reform 1 outlaws rebates, eliminating this leakage by design.

Second, even when PBMs secure discounts, those discounts reach only a subset of patients. Many face list or near-list prices regardless of what the plan paid net. Patients in the deductible phase pay the full negotiated rate until the deductible is met. Specialty-drug patients on coinsurance pay a percentage of list, not net, absorbing the gross-to-net spread the plan paid (Lakdawalla and Li 2021). Out-of-network and emergency patients face near-list rates. Cash payers using third-party discount tools sometimes pay less than their own insurance would charge for the same drug at the same pharmacy. Small employers and small commercial insurers pay higher rates than large ones. Patients prescribed off-formulary drugs pay above-formulary rates. Under the Bloc, the negotiated price is the price every patient pays.

Third, the three large PBMs are corporately integrated with the three large commercial insurers (CVS Caremark with Aetna, Express Scripts with Cigna, OptumRx with UnitedHealth). A PBM bargains for rebates on the plan's behalf and decides what share of those rebates to pass through. When the PBM and the plan share a corporate parent, the pass-through ratio is set by intra-corporate accounting rather than arm's-length negotiation, and the parent retains revenue regardless of how the split is drawn (FTC 2024). The Bloc has no such conflict. It has no sister insurer to favor and no separate revenue line tied to retaining rebates.

Fourth, PBMs compete with each other for plan business, which constrains how aggressively each can push back against any one manufacturer. The Bloc has no competing alternative and cannot be replaced as a buyer. Under Reform 2, a manufacturer that refuses the Bloc forfeits access to Bloc-contracted pharmacies, which constitute the majority of US retail, and its outside option shrinks to a small specialty channel.

The empirical record supports these reasons. Every other developed country negotiates drug prices through some form of centralized authority and secures substantially lower prices than the US for the same drugs. For the ten drugs in the first round of Medicare's IRA negotiations, US post-rebate net prices remained well above prices paid in other developed countries, even after the IRA's negotiated reductions (Wouters et al. 2025). For drugs outside the IRA-negotiated set, the gaps are typically larger. Each of these national health systems is much smaller than the US market, yet each individually achieves lower prices than US payers achieve through PBM-mediated bargaining. A US-wide Bloc would be the gatekeeper of the largest and most profitable pharmaceutical market in the world, a buyer several times the size of any single European negotiator. Given its size and leverage, the Bloc should at minimum match the prices these countries achieve, and likely beat them.

Why must the Bloc cover everything, including comparatively priced services such as generics?

Universal scope is different from universal action. The Bloc would be granted authority to negotiate all healthcare products and services, even those that are reasonably and comparatively priced, such as generic drugs. It would not be required to negotiate every price. Where competition produces low prices on its own, the Bloc need not act. US generic drugs trade at among the lowest prices in the developed world (Wouters et al. 2017), and the Bloc would have no reason to disturb these functioning and competitive corners of the market. Where competition fails, the Bloc exerts its leverage. Single-source branded drugs, biosimilars facing entry barriers, and hospital services in concentrated markets are the natural targets.

Statutory exemptions are predictable points of failure. If generics are carved out, manufacturers have an incentive to reformulate existing drugs into the exempt category to escape negotiation. If a therapeutic class is carved out, pricing pressure migrates into it, and the carve-out absorbs the abuse it was meant to prevent. Comprehensive authority closes both channels at once, and lets the Bloc decide where to act on the merits rather than where the statute permits.

The Bloc need not negotiate every price for its authority to bind every price. The credible possibility of negotiation is enough to discipline manufacturers whose drugs sit outside current focus. This is the standard logic of regulatory deterrence. Antitrust, food and drug safety, and securities enforcement operate this way. Most firms are never sued, audited, or investigated, but the credible threat of action disciplines behavior across the market. Reform 1 applies that logic to pricing.

How does the Bloc set drug prices and dispensing fees?

The patient's drug price has two components, and the Bloc sets each through a separate process. It negotiates the price of the drug itself with the manufacturer, with leverage and mechanics described in the FAQ on PBM comparison. It sets the dispensing fee paid to the retail pharmacy from cost-of-dispensing surveys, on a published, periodically updated schedule.

Regulating two price levels in a vertical supply chain can invite double marginalization, where a markup at one stage is stacked on top of a markup at the stage before it, so the two compound. PBM rebates and pharmacy reimbursement work this way today, with the gap accruing to the PBM (Sood et al. 2017; Spengler 1950; Tirole 1988). The Bloc avoids this because the dispensing fee is set to the cost of filling the prescription, not levied as a markup on the drug price. The fee is a fixed amount for each prescription filled, so a patient picking up several drugs generates a fee for each one. The fee is driven by the work of dispensing rather than the value of the drug, so a pharmacy that helps a patient switch to a lower-priced drug earns the same fee and is never paid less for saving the patient money. The mechanism at each level then follows from where the pricing power lies. The drug price is negotiated, because that is where buyer leverage matters, and the dispensing fee is set from surveyed cost, because no individual pharmacy has the scarcity power to bargain one up.

Per-prescription dispensing cost is driven by volume, not by what the pharmacy pays in rent. Filling a prescription costs a low-volume pharmacy substantially more (Grant Thornton 2007). So a uniform nationwide dispensing fee is unsuitable. To explain, a Manhattan pharmacy pays steep rent, yet fills so many prescriptions that its cost per prescription stays low. A rural Nebraska pharmacy pays little rent, but with so few prescriptions to spread it over, its cost per prescription is high. The solution is a national base fee from periodic independent cost-of-dispensing surveys, plus a rural premium based on RUCA codes that rises gradually as an area gets more rural rather than jumping at a fixed cutoff. Because RUCA codes are assigned externally from Census geography, not reported by the pharmacy, there is no input a pharmacy can manipulate to inflate its own premium. And because the premium rises smoothly with how rural an area is, rather than jumping at a threshold, two nearby pharmacies are paid nearly the same, even if they are in different RUCA codes. So arbitrary relocation across RUCA boundaries would not create a sizeable windfall.

Two further adjustments operate at the level of the individual prescription. Specialty drugs and compounded prescriptions carry prescription-level surcharges. For a given drug, the fee attaches to a standard days-supply, so a pharmacy cannot multiply its fees by splitting a single ninety-day prescription into three thirty-day pickups.

Alternative designs fall short. A uniform national fee is administratively simple but underprovides rural access, accelerating closures of independent rural pharmacies. Property-price tiers compensate in the wrong direction, since volume drives cost. Per-pharmacy negotiated fees disadvantage independent pharmacies that lack the data and legal capacity to negotiate against chains, replicating the PBM-pharmacy reimbursement dynamics Reform 1 is meant to undo.

Where the rural premium is insufficient to keep the only pharmacy in town open, the response is access policy (HRSA Pharmacy Access grants, telepharmacy, mail-order coverage), not a pricing premium that would create incentives to monopolize the local market. This follows Australia's Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme and the UK NHS pharmacy reimbursement system, and resembles several US state Medicaid programs.

Isn't this price-fixing?

No. Each seller chooses its own price. What the proposal enforces is non-discrimination: whatever price a provider sets, the provider must charge it to every patient regardless of who is paying. Sellers are not required to contract with the Bloc; any provider unwilling to accept the terms is free to walk away. The proposal sets no price; it conditions access to a large pool of publicly-funded patients on a single, transparent rate.

Isn't this central planning?

No. Centrally planned systems dictate what services are provided, in what quantities, to whom, and how resources are allocated. This proposal does none of that. It does not create rationing, waiting lists, or regional allocation. It does not tell providers what to produce or which patients to treat. It does not replace the market for insurance with a government plan; insurers remain distinct entities competing on coverage and cost-sharing. The market thrives. Price transparency promotes a more competitive and efficient market. What disappears is secret prices that result in information asymmetry, inefficiency, and exploitation.

Is this all-payer pricing?

It shares the core principle of all-payer pricing: at any given provider, every payer pays the same price. The differences are in scope and enforcement. Maryland's all-payer system is mandatory but covers hospitals only. Reform 2 covers all provider types (hospitals, physicians, pharmacies) but participation is voluntary. A provider that does not contract with the Bloc remains free to set different prices for different payers, but forfeits access to the Bloc's patient pool. Maryland enforces non-discrimination through state regulation. Reform 2 enforces it through a contract condition.

Why is US price discrimination different from textbook price discrimination?

Standard analysis of price discrimination predicts that the seller sorts buyers by price sensitivity and charges the more price-sensitive buyer less. When the more price-sensitive buyer is also lower-income, the result can be welfare-increasing and equity-improving (Varian 1985; Tirole 1988). The result depends on a specific condition. The price-sensitive buyer must be able to credibly walk away from a high price.

US healthcare prices are not set by sensitivity. They are set by negotiation leverage. Buyers with leverage (Medicare, the VA, large commercial insurers) extract low prices. Buyers with less leverage face high ones. If the patients most sensitive to price also have the least leverage, the standard sorting is inverted, and the most price-sensitive end up paying the most. The condition holds in each of the following cases.

The uninsured, typically lower-income and most price-sensitive at the point of care, face list prices that run several multiples of negotiated rates because they have little leverage in the individual transaction (Bai and Anderson 2015; Dusetzina et al. 2015). Insured patients in the deductible phase pay the full negotiated rate out of pocket until the deductible is met (averaging over $1,700 in 2023). They cannot credibly walk away from prescribed treatment, and the plan's leverage does not reach them at the register (KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey 2024). Specialty-drug patients on coinsurance pay a percentage of list rather than of the post-rebate net, absorbing the gross-to-net spread because they have little leverage to renegotiate the basis of their cost-sharing (Lakdawalla and Li 2021). Out-of-network and emergency patients default to near-list rates because they cannot credibly walk away from urgent care and have limited leverage with the provider (Adler et al. 2021). Insured patients sometimes pay more for the same drug at the same pharmacy than uninsured buyers using third-party discount cards. Roughly 23% of insured prescriptions in one large sample carried a copay above the cash price, showing that plan leverage does not consistently flow through (Van Nuys et al. 2018). Small employers and small commercial insurers pay higher hospital rates than large ones for the same service because they have less leverage (Cooper et al. 2019). In each case the people with the least bargaining power, not the least need, pay the most. So charging everyone the same negotiated price ends a form of price discrimination that here reduces welfare and worsens equity instead of improving them.

Hospital care is often paid in bundles, not per service. Can prices still be transparent?

Yes. Bundled payment and price transparency are independent design choices. Bundling, including the DRG system used by Medicare for inpatient hospital stays, was developed as a cost-control tool. It pays a fixed amount per episode of care rather than per item, transferring efficiency risk to the provider and removing the incentive to over-test and over-treat. It has nothing to do with whether prices are visible.

Several developed countries publish DRG payment schedules. A patient in Germany, France, Japan, or Australia can look up what their hospital is paid for, say, an uncomplicated knee replacement before being admitted. The bundle itself is the priced unit, and that priced unit is published. The same approach works under this proposal: each bundled episode appears in the lookup system as a single price, just as each individual fee-for-service item appears as a single price. Whether the contracted unit is a CPT code, a DRG, a bundle, or a formula, the rule is the same: if the contract sets the price, the database posts the price.

Can high-earning specialists simply drop Medicare and opt out of the Bloc entirely?

Some will. A concierge orthopedic surgeon or dermatologist serving an exclusively wealthy clientele can exit the system, just as such providers do today. The proposal does not depend on retaining them. The overwhelming majority of providers, including nearly all primary care physicians, general surgeons, and community hospitals, cannot afford to forfeit access to the Bloc's patient pool. Those who opt out serve a luxury market with negligible effect on the system's overall cost structure.

What happens to rural hospitals and safety-net providers that depend on higher commercial rates to stay open?

Under the Bloc, each provider negotiates its own rate. A rural hospital with high fixed costs and low patient volume will negotiate a higher rate. It has genuine leverage: the Bloc cannot leave its beneficiaries with zero access in a region served by a single hospital. Transparent negotiation yields a sustainable price. The hospital does not close; it charges what it needs to and justifies that price openly. Where additional support is warranted, existing mechanisms such as Critical Access Hospital designations and Disproportionate Share Hospital payments provide direct subsidies, and these can be maintained or adjusted without preserving a broken pricing system.

Can drug manufacturers credibly threaten to withdraw from the US market?

No. The United States is the most profitable pharmaceutical market in the world. Every other developed nation negotiates drug prices through centralized purchasing and pays a fraction of US prices. A manufacturer that withdraws from the US market to avoid consolidated negotiation would be abandoning its highest-margin customers in favor of markets that pay far less. This is a claim made by industry lobbyists, not a credible economic threat. For the vast majority of drugs, withdrawal is irrational. For niche drugs with very small patient populations, targeted accommodations can be made without abandoning the overall pricing framework.

Would lower drug prices reduce pharmaceutical research and innovation?

Only modestly, and mostly among drugs that add little therapeutic benefit (Acemoglu and Linn 2004; Dubois et al. 2015). The CBO's drug-development simulation suggests that even a sizeable drop in profitability creates a modest decline in new drug entry, and it mostly affects low-value projects (CBO 2021b). Only 10 to 13% of new approvals offer a real clinical advantage over existing options (Light and Lexchin 2012; Naci, Carter, and Mossialos 2015), so the effect falls largely on me-too drugs. Public funding underwrites the upstream science. NIH research contributed to every one of the 210 new drugs approved between 2010 and 2016 (Cleary et al. 2018). And every other developed country negotiates drug prices, yet pharmaceutical innovation continues across those markets. The claim that negotiation would halt innovation is not supported by the evidence.

What is the net fiscal impact on the federal budget?

Total healthcare spending is very likely to fall. The Bloc is larger than any single existing program, and larger insurers pay lower prices. Commercial rates, which currently exceed Medicare rates by multiples, would collapse to the negotiated rate. The savings to employers, patients, and state Medicaid budgets would be substantial.

The federal budget effect is theoretically ambiguous, with two opposing forces. The downward force is the gain from negotiating as a single large buyer rather than a dozen fragmented ones. The empirical record shows this gain can be enormous: even modest exercises of consolidated buying power, such as the VA's drug-price negotiation or the limited Medicare negotiation under the IRA, have produced price reductions on the order of 50%. The upward force is that some providers, once they can no longer cross-subsidize from commercial payers, may demand higher rates from the Bloc than they accept from Medicare alone. The downward force is likely to dominate, because the consolidation gain applies to the full federal portfolio while only a minority of providers genuinely depend on commercial cross-subsidies to break even. The most plausible outcome is a meaningful net reduction in federal healthcare spending alongside the larger system-wide reduction. The precise magnitude is an empirical question that future research could investigate.
